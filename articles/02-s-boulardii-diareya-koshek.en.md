## Common Causes of Diarrhea in Cats

Diarrhea in cats is a polyetiological condition, and treatment choice depends on the cause. The most common presentations include:

- Infectious agents: bacteria (Clostridium perfringens, E. coli), viruses (feline coronavirus), protozoa (Giardia).
    
- Intestinal dysbiosis after antibiotics or stress.
    
- Dietary factors: abrupt diet change, food intolerance, poor-quality diet.
    
- Chronic GI diseases (IBD, pancreatitis).
    
- Parasitic infestations.
    

In practice, searching for "cat diarrhea treatment" often leads to symptomatic therapy, but without microbiota correction, the effect is unstable. This is where the "probiotic for cat diarrhea" approach applies.

Scientific data confirm that microbiota disruption is a key factor in many forms of diarrhea in animals.

## How the Probiotic S. boulardii Works

Saccharomyces boulardii is not a bacterial but a yeast probiotic, which determines its unique properties and resistance to antibiotics.

The mechanisms of action of S. boulardii for cats include:

- Antagonism toward pathogens: suppression of Clostridium and E. coli growth through competition and metabolite production.
    
- Toxin inactivation (especially bacterial enterotoxins).
    
- Enhancement of intestinal barrier function (tight junctions).
    
- Immunomodulation (stimulation of IgA and anti-inflammatory pathways).
    
- Restoration of microbiota after antibiotics.
    

These effects are described in detail in systematic reviews and experimental studies.

Importantly, S. boulardii does not permanently colonize the gut but acts transiently, making it a safe and controllable tool.

In veterinary practice, probiotics for cat diarrhea have been shown to reduce diarrhea duration and improve stool consistency, particularly in infectious and antibiotic-associated forms.

## Practical Use in Cats

When is S. boulardii used for cats:

- Acute diarrhea (including infectious).
    
- Post-antibiotic diarrhea.
    
- Support for chronic enteropathies.
    
- Stress-induced GI disorders.
    

Typical approach (general, not replacing veterinary prescription):

- 5–10 day course for acute diarrhea.
    
- Combination with rehydration.
    
- When needed — alongside sorbents and diet.
    

Veterinary sources note that probiotics, including S. boulardii, are part of comprehensive therapy, not a replacement for diagnostic workup of the underlying cause.

## When a Veterinarian Is Needed

Despite the popularity of the search term "probiotic for cat diarrhea", there are situations where self-treatment is dangerous:

- Diarrhea lasting more than 2–3 days.
    
- Blood in the stool.
    
- Lethargy, refusal to eat.
    
- Kittens and elderly animals.
    
- Suspected viral infection.
    

In these cases, diagnostics (fecal analysis, PCR, biochemistry) are required, and the probiotic is used as an adjunct.
